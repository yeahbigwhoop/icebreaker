Regular Council Meeting — March 6, 2026
Mar 6, 2026 watch video ↗ transcript ↗

A dense session running 9:08 a.m.–5:37 p.m. The headline: the council voted unanimously (0–9) to reject Bill 92 — which would have authorized a new FBI Joint Terrorism Task Force intergovernmental agreement — then immediately passed Reso 26-56 (8–0–1) urging the Mayor to terminate the existing 2007 MPD-FBI JTTF agreement. On housing, Bill 116 CD1 cleared first reading unanimously: it would exempt below-market affordable housing leases from competitive bidding requirements and Council approval, while adding annual reporting obligations. Final readings completed on the $10M DWS water system integration and $3M IT/cybersecurity bills first read in February. First readings opened on a batch of budget amendments covering emergency management reallocation, War Memorial Gym repairs ($800K), parks operations ($550K), and federal housing grants (HOME-ARP, ESG, CDBG). The council also adopted resolutions urging state action on the Pāʻia Bypass and a ban on commercial aquarium harvesting, and urged denial of the Air Force's Haleakalā telescope EIS. Bill 34 (Kamalani Ventures LLC affordable housing repayment) was cancelled by the clerk without reaching a floor vote.

⚑ Worth Watching
  • FBI JTTF (Bill 92 failed / Reso 26-56 passed): The council rejected the new JTTF IGA 0–9, then urged the Mayor to terminate the existing 2007 agreement 8–0–1. Reso 26-56 is precatory — the Mayor controls executive IGAs — but the signal is unanimous. Worth reviewing the legal mechanism for IGA termination, any required federal notice, and potential impacts on information-sharing arrangements or mutual aid obligations under federal law.
  • Bill 116 CD1 — lease exemptions (first reading): Amends MCC 3.36 and 3.40 to bypass competitive bidding and Council approval for certain affordable housing leases. Charter §8-8 compliance review is warranted before second reading, particularly whether the County may delegate or waive Council oversight of property dispositions by ordinance.
  • Bill 34 cancellation (Kamalani Ventures LLC): This bill — adding conditional repayment language for Kamalani Ventures LLC in the Affordable Housing Fund — was pulled at the clerk level, not tabled or deferred. The reason is unstated in the transcript. Worth ascertaining whether a dispute, negotiation, or litigation is pending with Kamalani Ventures, and whether the cancellation affects any contractual obligation to seek repayment.
  • Bill 38 — General Plan ordinance amendment (first reading, as amended): Amends MCC Chapter 2.80B governing the General Plan and Community Plans process. Changes here can affect standing, timelines, and scope of review in land use challenges. Watch for the amendment text and second reading.
  • Resos 26-57/58 — grant fund reassignment to Kaiahale Kahiluhilu LPs: Affordable housing grants transferred from Hui Kauhale, Inc. to two new limited partnerships. Worth confirming that grant assignment documentation is complete, any prior security interests transfer correctly, and that the County's compliance obligations under the original grant follow the new entities.
↻ Coming Up

April 2 agenda already posted. Second readings pending for Bills 38, 27 CD1, 14, 116 CD1, 39, 44, 46, 40, 41, 42, 45, and 43. Note: two emergency meetings were held March 20 to address $15M in storm-recovery appropriations (Bills 53 & 54) — Worth confirming those emergency procedures complied with HRS §92-3.7 notice requirements. Bill 34 (Kamalani Ventures) may return. FY2027 budget season is now open.

Regular Council Meeting — February 18, 2026
Feb 18, 2026 watch video ↗ transcript ↗

Twenty-three board and commission appointments were confirmed — one of the larger single-meeting batches of the term. The council passed Bill 183 (6–1–2) on second and final reading, amending the West Maui Community Plan to set a 30-foot height limit for the Lāhainā Historic Landmark District, a key post-fire rebuilding constraint. First readings cleared for the $10M DWS water system integration bill and $3M IT/cybersecurity bill, both of which returned for final passage on March 6. The county accepted a donation of four Compact Rapid Deployable cell towers for fire and public safety use. Bill 17 (repealing social host liability provisions) passed its final reading, reducing a category of civil exposure for the county.

⚑ Worth Watching
  • Bill 183 — Lāhainā 30-ft height limit (6–1–2): The slim margin and two excused members are worth noting. A blanket height restriction codified in a community plan amendment may be challenged by affected property owners on takings grounds or as inconsistent with state land use law or SMA requirements. Worth assessing litigation exposure and confirm the legislative record contains adequate findings supporting the restriction. A future reconsideration motion could succeed if attendance shifts.
  • 23 board and commission appointments: Volume creates statistical likelihood that at least one appointment deviated from charter or MCC procedural requirements. Routine spot-check is advisable, particularly for boards with quasi-judicial functions (e.g., Board of Variances and Appeals, Board of Ethics) where appointment defects could affect the validity of subsequent decisions.
  • Cell tower donation acceptance: Accepted under MCC 3.56.030. Worth confirming the donation agreement clearly limits County liability for equipment failure in the field, establishes no ongoing maintenance obligation by the donor, and does not create a commercial relationship that would require competitive procurement for future support.
  • Bill 17 (social host liability repeal) — enacted: Removal of social host liability provisions reduces one litigation exposure category. Verify effective date and whether any claims filed under the prior provision remain active and how repeal affects them.
Regular Council Meeting — February 6, 2026
Feb 6, 2026 watch video ↗ transcript ↗

The meeting opened with recognition of Kamehameha Schools Maui football's second consecutive state championship. Bill 15 passed final reading, authorizing factory-built housing in the Lāhainā burn zone — with a last-minute floor amendment requiring that the manufacturing facilities themselves be Hawaii-based. A $10M budget amendment for the Kula Agriculture Park and $886K in opioid settlement expenditures were approved. Kehalani Apartments received affordable housing exemptions enabling a May 2026 construction start. Hale Pilina grant funding was redistributed to keep that project on track. Bill 2 (Board of Ethics salary reform) and Bill 17 (social host liability repeal) each had their first readings.

⚑ Worth Watching
  • Bill 15 — Hawaii-only factory requirement (enacted): The floor amendment requiring that factory-built housing manufacturers be Hawaii-based is a significant dormant Commerce Clause risk. Geographic preferences for in-state businesses over out-of-state competitors are subject to strict scrutiny under the U.S. Constitution and have frequently been struck down. A formal legal opinion on the provision's defensibility would be prudent before the County invests in program implementation.
  • Opioid settlement funds ($886K): These expenditures must comply with the use restrictions defined in the applicable settlement agreement and any associated abatement plan. Worth verifying that approved uses match the settlement terms and that documentation is maintained for required reporting to the settlement administrator.
  • Kehalani Apartments — May 2026 construction deadline: The exemptions granted are conditioned on a May 2026 construction start. If that deadline is missed, the legal basis for the exemptions may lapse and the project could require reapproval. Flag for monitoring.
  • Bill 2 — Board of Ethics compensation (first reading): Compensating Board of Ethics members raises questions about charter intent (the board is typically designed as an independent volunteer citizen body), potential conflicts of interest for compensated members, and whether compensation is authorized without a charter amendment. Watch for second reading.
